🧭 The Story — A Vindication of the Plan

After the "insipid" Morocco draw, this was the convincing, confidence-restoring win Brazil needed — and it arrived precisely through the fixes the opener cried out for. Ancelotti dropped Igor Thiago for Matheus Cunha, and it paid off immediately: the Manchester United forward scored twice and, as a more mobile false-nine type, dropped and interchanged with Vinícius and Raphinha to unlock Haiti's five-man defence. Haiti didn't help themselves — they defended too high, and the pace of Brazil's front three repeatedly exposed the space in behind. By half-time it was 3-0 and over. Brazil are top of Group C on goal difference, and Haiti became the first nation eliminated from the tournament.

✅ The pre-match fixes, vindicated

Our Brazil pre-match prescription was twofold: (1) start a sharper, more mobile No.9 (Cunha for Thiago), and (2) fill the box so the wingers' work finds bodies. Both came true almost to the letter. Cunha's opener was literally the "fill the box" fix in action — he was the body in the six-yard area to bury the rebound from Vinícius's saved shot, the exact pattern that found nobody against Morocco. And his mobility from the No.9 role gave Brazil the fluid, interchanging front line they'd lacked. The plan worked.

⚽ Goal 1 (23') — Cunha, the body in the box

Brazil · 1-0The rebound striker — exactly the missing piece
Vinícius shot (saved) Keeper's spill Cunha finish

The goal that proved the point. Vinícius fired a shot that Johnny Placide couldn't hold, and Cunha was right there to turn home the rebound — the deserved opener after 20 minutes of patient probing. (He was slightly fortunate it stood, having caught a defender in the act.) But the tactical lesson is the headline: this is the exact situation that produced nothing against Morocco, when the box was empty on Brazil's entries. Start a striker whose instinct is to gamble in the six-yard area, and saved shots become goals. Cunha was the missing body — and Brazil's first goal of the tournament from open play besides Vini's solo effort came directly from fixing it.

⚽ Goal 2 (36') — Cunha's stunner off the Vini steal

Brazil · 2-0Win it high, slide it through, smash the roof of the net
Vinícius steal & through-ball Cunha first-time finish

The pick of the goals — and a glimpse of the fluid front line. Vinícius snatched possession in midfield, then immediately played the role of creator, sliding the ball through for Cunha to lash a first-time finish high into the roof of the net as he fell away off-balance. It captured exactly why Ancelotti's selection worked: with Cunha as a mobile, interchanging No.9 rather than a static target, Brazil's front three (Vini, Cunha, Raphinha) fluidly swapped roles — provider one moment, finisher the next — and Haiti's high line had no way to track the rotations. This is the cohesion that was utterly absent against Morocco.

⚽ Goal 3 (45+3') — Vini from provider to scorer

Brazil · 3-0Paquetá's lofted pass & the 1-on-1 finish
Paquetá's lofted pass Vinícius run & finish

The third that killed it before the break. Having created the first goal, Vinícius switched from playmaker to predator: he latched onto Lucas Paquetá's lofted pass over the top, controlled it, and slid the ball under the onrushing Placide in first-half stoppage time. The goal is a textbook punishment of a high line — exactly the flaw Haiti showed all half. It also underlined Paquetá's influential display (he was excellent in his 64 minutes) and capped a near-perfect 45 minutes. Brazil eased off after the break, content with the job done.

⚠️ The Honest Caveats

Read the result, not just the scoreline
  • Eight shots is alarmingly few for a 3-0 — the joint-fourth fewest by any side to score 3+ in a World Cup game since 1966. Brazil were ruthless, but the volume of chance-creation against a weak side was modest.
  • Haiti were ranked 78 places below Brazil — the biggest gap in any group at this World Cup, and they defended too high. Far tougher tests await; this tells us less than it seems.
  • Raphinha limped off at 40' with an apparent hamstring injury — a genuine concern for the knockouts, with Neymar still only just returning from his calf problem.

🔧 Future Fix 1 — Keep the mobile front line vs better defences

Brazil · Fix 1Cunha's interchange must survive against a deep, disciplined block
Vini run / Bruno feed Cunha drop & lay-off CB tempted out → gap

The pattern to preserve. The reason Cunha worked wasn't just finishing — it was his movement. Against Haiti's high line, dropping deep let Brazil's pace run in behind. But Scotland and the knockout sides will defend deep and disciplined, denying that space. The same mechanism still works if applied differently: Cunha drops between the lines to drag a centre-back out of the block — if the defender follows, a gap opens for Vinícius (or Rayan) to attack; if he doesn't, Cunha receives free to turn and combine. Brazil must keep this fluid, interchanging front line rather than reverting to a static target man. The cohesion, not the opponent's generosity, is what to carry forward — because against a deep block, only the movement creates the chances.

🛡️ Future Fix 2 — Sharpen the midfield & cover for injuries

Brazil · Fix 2More chance volume + depth as Raphinha/Neymar fitness looms
Lay-off to midfield Late run + shot from range Winger cut-in

Two related concerns for the knockouts. First, eight shots is too few — against better defences, Brazil need more sustained chance-creation, and the obvious lever is the midfield: Bruno Guimarães and Paquetá arriving late into the box and shooting from range adds volume and forces a deep block to step out (the same idea that would help Spain). Relying on a clinical front three to convert a handful of chances is fine against Haiti, dangerous against a Croatia or a France. Second, and more pressing: depth. Raphinha's hamstring injury and Neymar's slow return from his calf leave the creative supply thinner than it looks — the emergence of teenagers Rayan and Endrick (who made his debut) is timely, but Ancelotti needs his front-line fitness to hold. Brazil look back in business; whether they're contenders depends on raising the chance volume and surviving the injuries.

🗺️ What Happens Next

Job done, confidence restored, top of the group on goal difference — but with a twist: Group C is still wide open, and top spot will be decided on the final day against Scotland in Miami, with Neymar expected back in the lineup. The Haiti win answered the most urgent questions (Cunha works, the front line can be fluid, the box can be filled), but the eight-shot tally and Raphinha's injury mean the bigger questions about chance-volume and depth remain open. Brazil are back in business — but "not quite" contenders yet, as ESPN put it.

📊 Bottom Line

The response Brazil needed, delivered exactly as prescribed: a sharper No.9 in Cunha, a fluid interchanging front line, and bodies in the box that turned the Morocco frustration into a ruthless three-goal first half. Ancelotti's big call paid off and the campaign is alive. But read the result honestly — eight shots against a side 78 places below them, and a hamstring scare for Raphinha — and the work isn't done. Against Scotland's organised block and the knockouts beyond, Brazil must raise the chance-volume and prove their depth. The fixes worked against Haiti; the real test is whether they hold against teams that don't defend so high.
