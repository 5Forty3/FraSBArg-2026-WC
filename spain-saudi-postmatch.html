🧭 The Story — From Frustration to Statement

If the Cape Verde draw asked every question about Spain, this answered them in 24 minutes. La Roja were clinical, technically immaculate, and looked every inch champions — the first half-hour, three goals and all, was the most dominant spell any side has produced at this World Cup. They toyed with Saudi Arabia, winning every loose ball and making the elite look effortless. The single change that unlocked everything was the one the opener demanded: De la Fuente bet on dribblers and width. Starting Yamal, with Dani Olmo and Álex Baena providing genuine width, fixed the central congestion that plagued Match 1 — where Pedri, Gavi, Torres and the rest had all crowded the same pockets playing the same way. This time the pitch was stretched, the lanes were defined, and the quality poured through.

✅ Every pre-match fix, delivered

The prescription was exact, and so was the execution. Start the game-breakers → Yamal started and scored his first World Cup goal inside 10 minutes, drawing up to three markers all game. Width instead of central congestion → Olmo and Baena stretched the field, with Olmo outstanding in a central role. Fill the box / find a poacher → Mikel Oyarzabal answered the years-old "no true No.9" question with a clinical brace and an assist, Player of the Match. From 27 shots and 0 goals to 20 shots and 4 goals — the dam didn't just break, it burst.

⚽ Goal 1 (10') — Yamal's first World Cup goal

Spain · 1-0Oyarzabal's cross, Yamal arrives
Oyarzabal cross Yamal run & finish

The fix made flesh inside ten minutes. The roles were a perfect inversion of the team's identity crisis: the striker became the creator and the winger the finisher. Oyarzabal delivered the cross from the left, and Lamine Yamal arrived to score his first-ever World Cup goal at just 18 — the spark Spain had been crying out for. With Saudi defending deep and offering space, and Olmo/Baena stretching the pitch, Yamal had room to attack the back post rather than being swallowed in traffic. The early goal calmed every Spanish nerve and opened the floodgates — a world away from the anxious, goalless probing against Cape Verde.

⚽ Goal 2 (21') — Oyarzabal, the poacher's instinct

Spain · 2-0Laporte keeps it alive, Oyarzabal pounces
Laporte keeps the attack alive Oyarzabal close-range finish

The goal that answered Spain's oldest question. For years the great Spanish sides have lacked a true penalty-box predator. Here was the antidote: when Aymeric Laporte kept a Spanish attack alive in the box, Oyarzabal was perfectly positioned to finish from close range — the instinctive movement and clinical touch of a natural poacher. It's exactly the profile that was missing against Cape Verde, when crosses sailed into an empty area. Oyarzabal occupying the six-yard space, gambling on the loose ball, and converting without hesitation is the piece of the puzzle Spanish football has chased for a decade.

⚽ Goal 3 (24') — Oyarzabal's volley off Olmo's header

Spain · 3-0Cucurella cross, Olmo's knockdown, Oyarzabal volleys home
Cucurella cross Olmo header Oyarzabal volley

The pick of the three — and a showcase of the new width. This was the width fix scoring a goal: Marc Cucurella delivered from the left, Dani Olmo rose to head it across, and Oyarzabal volleyed home his second to make it 3-0 inside 24 minutes. Three things the opener lacked, all present at once — a genuine wide delivery, a body attacking the cross, and a finisher arriving with conviction. Olmo was exceptional in his central role all afternoon (he had a hand in two goals), the perfect link between Pedri's orchestration and Oyarzabal's finishing. This was the most dominant 24-minute spell of the tournament so far.

📋 Performance Snapshot

Mikel Oyarzabal — Player of the Match. Two goals and an assist. The opportunistic poacher Spain have lacked for a decade — and a clever creator who draws defenders to free others.
Lamine Yamal — Unplayable. First World Cup goal, a constant right-side threat, drew up to three markers all game. Managed off at half-time with the job done.
Dani Olmo — Exceptional. Outstanding in the central role, involved in two goals, the link that made the front line flow.
Pedri — Chief orchestrator. Back to his best, controlling the tempo and stringing together 20+ pass sequences in the Saudi third.
Álex Baena — Width & set-pieces. Provided the genuine left-side width missing in Match 1; his corner led to the fourth.
Unai Simón — A spectator. Untested across two games; Saudi's only shot on target was a tame late effort. The defence remains unbreached.

🔧 Future Tactic 1 — Keep this shape, but tested by a real opponent

Spain · Future 1Width + a poacher vs a side that won't gift space
Width → service The fixed No.9 attacks it

The blueprint is found — now protect it. The lesson of these two games is stark: the shape that produced 0 goals and the shape that produced 4 differ only in discipline of roles. Spain must keep this structure — wingers genuinely wide (Yamal right, Nico/Williams left), one designated No.10 (Olmo), and a fixed poacher (Oyarzabal) gambling in the box — rather than drifting back to four players in the same central pocket. The honest caveat: Saudi gifted Spain space by attacking and defending too high. Uruguay won't. Bielsa's side will be compact and aggressive, and the width-plus-poacher pattern will have to break a disciplined block that doesn't hand over the lanes. The personnel now fit the problem; the next test is whether the pattern works when space must be created, not merely found.

⏳ Future Tactic 2 — Manage Yamal's minutes & sharpen the second-half tempo

Spain · Future 2Don't let the game go flat when the stars rest

The one refinement from a near-perfect day. Once Spain hit four and rotated — Yamal and Oyarzabal off, Nico Williams, Merino and Pino on — the tempo noticeably dropped and the game went flat. Against Saudi that was harmless game-management; the points were long secure. But two things to carry forward: first, Yamal still can't complete 90 minutes, so the half-time hook will recur, and Spain must learn to sustain their level without him for spells — crucial in a knockout when he might be needed late. Second, the second-string must keep the intensity up rather than coasting, because against Uruguay (or in a tight knockout) there'll be no comfortable lead to coast on. The depth is there — Nico Williams shifting Cucurella into a midfield role created overloads even late — but the habit of relentlessness for 90 minutes is the final piece. A brilliant day; this is the fine-tuning.

🗺️ What Happens Next

Qualification to the Round of 32 is sealed, the attacking blueprint is found, and the defence still hasn't conceded — or barely been tested — across two games. Spain top Group H and head into the final-day decider against Bielsa's Uruguay likely playing for top spot. That is a different animal entirely: organised, intense, and unwilling to gift the space Saudi did. It's the perfect examination of whether this clinical new Spain can break a disciplined block as easily as they punished a passive one. On today's evidence — Yamal flying, Oyarzabal finally giving them a killer, Olmo and Pedri purring — La Roja look every bit the champions they were billed as.

📊 Bottom Line

From the frustration of Cape Verde to a swaggering statement: Spain were clinical, technically immaculate, and looked like champions. Every fix the opener demanded was delivered — Yamal started and scored, Olmo and Baena gave the team width, and in Oyarzabal, Spain may finally have found the opportunistic No.9 that has eluded them for a generation. The 3-0 in 24 minutes was the tournament's most dominant spell. The only caveats are honest ones: Saudi gifted the space, and the tempo sagged once the stars rested. Uruguay will gift nothing. But the blueprint is found, the poacher has arrived, and the defence is yet to be breached — Spain are well and truly up and running.
