Jordan vs Argentina

The champions rest their legend — a night for the supporting cast to prove they can shine without Messi

🎯 The context — a free hit for the holders

This is a dead rubber with a twist of intrigue. Argentina have already won Group J with back-to-back clean-sheet wins over Algeria (3-0) and Austria (2-0), and are safely into the Round of 32 as group winners with a perfect record. Jordan, on their historic World Cup debut, are already eliminated — even a win would almost certainly not be enough. For the champions, this is a free hit: a chance to rest key legs, manage the workload before a likely R32 date with a Group H side (Cape Verde the probable opponent), and — most intriguingly — to see whether the team can function without the man who has scored all five of their goals so far.

📋 Confirmed by Scaloni: Messi starts on the bench

No guesswork on the headline question. In Friday's press conference, Lionel Scaloni confirmed Messi will start the game on the bench to rest his 39-year-old legs (he entered the tournament carrying an injury) ahead of the knockouts — while pointedly adding that "Leo will come on later," all but guaranteeing a second-half cameo. Per Argentine insider Gastón Edul, Messi himself is keen to feature, not wanting to go a full week without competitive rhythm before the R32. So tonight splits cleanly into two stories: can the supporting cast deliver without their talisman — and what changes the moment he steps on?

📋 Team News XIs predicted · Messi-on-bench confirmed

Beyond Messi (confirmed benched), the rest of the XI is predicted, not confirmed — Scaloni declined to reveal his hand and is expected to rotate heavily, so treat the outfield names as the likely shape rather than gospel.

🇦🇷 Argentina — the rotated XI (expected)

  • Messi benched (confirmed) — expected on for a second-half cameo. Nico González or a front two leads in his absence to start.
  • Julián Álvarez set for his first start — fitness rebuilt after an ankle injury, he's "ready to be unleashed." His increased influence is exactly the reduced-Messi-reliance the team needs, likely partnering Lautaro Martínez up top.
  • Romero OUT (knee, won't be risked after limping off vs Austria) — Otamendi steps in beside Lisandro Martínez, or three-cap Marcos Senesi gets a run.
  • A fresh midfield/attack: Leandro Paredes (deep tempo-setter), Exequiel Palacios, Giovani Lo Celso and Nico Paz are all in line for minutes — the creative depth that must replace Messi's spark.
  • Defence reshuffled: Montiel at right-back, Tagliafico at left-back; Emiliano Martínez likely retained in goal.

Expected XI (4-4-2): E. Martínez; Montiel, Otamendi, L. Martínez, Tagliafico; Nico Paz, Paredes, Palacios, Lo Celso; J. Álvarez, La. Martínez. (Messi, De Paul, Enzo, Mac Allister on the bench. Not confirmed.)

🇯🇴 Jordan — spirited debutants with nothing to lose

  • A deep, compact back five (3-4-3 / 5-4-1 in defence) under Jamal Sellami — the only realistic plan: protect the box, stay organised, and frustrate a much-changed Argentina.
  • Mousa Tamari is the danger — their most talented player and a genuine outlet on the counter; if Jordan threaten, it runs through him.
  • They have scored in both games (lost 3-1 to Austria, 2-1 to Algeria) — not a team that sits back entirely, and capable of a goal if Argentina's rotated, makeshift defence switches off.
  • The gulf is vast — World No. 1 vs No. 63, on Jordan's tournament debut. But a respectable showing, or even a goal, against the champions would be a fitting end to a historic campaign.

Projected XI (3-4-3): Abu Laila; Nasib, Al-Arab, Hussam Ali; Haddad, Al-Rashdan, Al-Rawabdeh, Abu Taha; Tamari, Olwan, Al-Mardi. (Not confirmed.)

⚽ Part 1 — Life Without Messi: Can the Cast Score?

This is the genuinely interesting tactical question, and a valuable rehearsal. Argentina have, by Scaloni's own evolution, tried to become more than a one-man team — but Messi has still scored all five of their goals. Tonight, against a deep block, the supporting cast must show they can break a team down and finish chances without the magician pulling the strings. Two patterns are key.

🔑 Without Messi 1 — A genuine two-striker threat: Álvarez & Lautaro

No-Messi · Pattern 1Direct vertical threat through a front two
Striker run in behind Vertical pass / late run

The most reliable Messi-free plan. Without their talisman to conjure the opening, Argentina's clearest route to goal is a genuine two-striker threat: pairing Julián Álvarez — likely making his first start of the tournament — with Lautaro Martínez, both running the channels of Jordan's back five rather than waiting to be fed. The key is verticality: Paredes drops to set the tempo and slide line-breaking passes, Palacios and Lo Celso time late runs into the box, and the scoring becomes positional and collective — runs, second balls, cut-backs — rather than dependent on one man's magic. Álvarez in particular has a point to prove (and his fitness to demonstrate), and a Lautaro who has looked sharp in flashes needs goals. This is the exact rehearsal Argentina want: proof the front line can break a low block on its own.

🌟 Without Messi 2 — Nico Paz & Lo Celso: the creative succession

No-Messi · Pattern 2A new creator pulls the strings between the lines
Nico Paz creation Striker run & finish

The succession audition — and the most exciting sub-plot. The night's real test is whether someone can fill the creative void Messi leaves, not just the goalscoring one. 21-year-old Nico Paz (a breakout Serie A season at Como) is the standout candidate: a No.10 who can receive between the lines, turn, and thread the defence-splitting pass that is normally Messi's department — plus a goal threat of his own from the edge of the box. Alongside him, Giovani Lo Celso finally gets the minutes he's been craving, operating in the left half-space to combine and create. Against a deep Jordan block, the pattern is patient probing then a moment of incision from Paz — exactly the kind of game in which a future creator announces himself. If Argentina are to win this World Cup, they may need someone other than Messi to unlock a packed defence on a tough night; tonight is the dress rehearsal.

👑 Part 2 — Enter the Legend: Messi as Super-Sub

Then comes the moment the AT&T Stadium crowd is waiting for. Scaloni has all but promised it — "Leo will come on later" — likely early in the second half, whether to chase a tighter-than-expected game or simply to top up his rhythm. The tactical picture transforms the instant he crosses the white line.

🎭 Super-Sub 1 — Fresh Messi vs tired legs: the game opens up

Messi · Super-SubA rested genius against a worn-down, stretched defence
Messi's killer pass Striker run / feed to Messi

The classic super-sub script — and why it's so dangerous. Introducing a fresh, rested Messi against defenders who have spent an hour chasing the ball in a Texas dome is the cruellest mismatch in football. Jordan's deep block relies on discipline and concentration; both fray as legs tire. The moment Messi arrives, he drifts into the pockets between a flagging defence and midfield, where exhausted markers simply can't get out to him in time — and from there his vision does the rest, slipping disguised through-balls for Álvarez and Lautaro to attack the space behind. Even 25–30 minutes of a rested Messi against tired opposition typically yields a goal or an assist; it's also the perfect low-risk way to sharpen his rhythm before the knockouts. For Jordan, having survived the first hour, the sight of him stripping off is the ultimate sting in the tail.

🎯 Super-Sub 2 — The record hunt: free-kicks & the Golden Boot

Messi · Super-SubA dead-ball master chasing more history
Messi free-kick

The bonus threat — and the personal incentives. Messi doesn't just create from open play; he's Argentina's primary free-kick, corner and penalty taker, so his introduction immediately upgrades every set-piece into a scoring chance — his curled dead-balls a constant menace against a tiring wall. And the motivations are real: already the all-time World Cup top scorer (18 goals), he leads the Golden Boot race with five, and every minute against the group's weakest defence is a chance to extend both records before the knockouts. Scaloni will manage the minutes carefully — there's no sense risking those 39-year-old legs in a dead rubber — but a cameo that yields a trademark free-kick or a tap-in would be the perfect, low-effort way to keep the history rolling. For the neutral, even 20 minutes of Messi is reason enough to watch.

🦂 Jordan's Hope — Tamari & a moment on the counter

It won't be entirely one-way. Jordan have scored in both their games and won't simply absorb pressure for 90 minutes — and a rotated, makeshift Argentine defence (Otamendi and possibly the three-cap Senesi, with reshuffled full-backs) could be vulnerable to a moment of quality. Mousa Tamari is the man: their most gifted player and a real threat in transition, capable of punishing any lapse in concentration. A goal against the champions on their World Cup debut would be the crowning memory of Jordan's historic campaign — and against a much-changed back line lacking its usual cohesion, it's not impossible. Argentina's rotated defenders must stay switched on.

📊 Verdict & Predicted Scoreline

A mismatch in all but motivation. Even heavily rotated and without Messi from the start, Argentina's depth — Álvarez, Lautaro, Nico Paz, Lo Celso — is the envy of the tournament and should be far too much for an eliminated Jordan side missing its best players' quality. The intrigue isn't the result but the manner: can the supporting cast break the block and finish without their talisman (a vital knockout rehearsal), and how much damage does a fresh Messi do when he arrives against tired legs. Expect Argentina to control it, find a goal or two through the front two or Nico Paz, then pull clear after Messi's introduction. Jordan will battle and may grab a consolation through Tamari, but the gulf is decisive.

🇯🇴 Jordan 0 — 3 Argentina

Most likely: a controlled first hour with a goal or two from the rotated XI, then a fresh Messi adds gloss late. A 1-1-at-some-point or a Jordan consolation via Tamari is very possible against a makeshift defence — but only one winner.
