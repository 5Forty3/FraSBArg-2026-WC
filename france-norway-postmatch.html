🧭 The Story — A First Half for the Ages, A Warning Underneath

For thirty-odd minutes, this was arguably the most dazzling attacking football of the tournament so far. France's transitions through midfield were simply breathtaking — fast, fluid, flooding forward with pace and flair that no defence on earth looks equipped to stop. At the heart of it was Ousmane Dembélé, who scored a stunning hat-trick inside 32 minutes — the second-fastest treble in World Cup history and the first first-half hat-trick since Salenko in 1994. He toyed with Norway: each goal a near-carbon-copy, cutting in from the right onto his left foot and finding the same corner, no matter how many bodies they threw at him. Mbappé hit the bar inside 22 seconds and was a menace all half; Olise, Mbappé and Dembélé combined so fluently they could have had six, repeatedly denied by a busy Norwegian keeper. And yet — France conceded, nearly conceded twice more, and the night carried a clear warning beneath the brilliance.

⚽ Goal 1 (7') — Mbappé's switch, Dembélé cuts in & finishes

France · 1-0The crossfield ball, the trademark cut inside, the far corner
Mbappé's crossfield switch Dembélé cut inside & finish

The opener that set the template. France struck early — and it began with Mbappé. His crossfield ball gave Dembélé the chance to cut in from the right and, in trademark fashion, flash a left-footed shot into the far corner, despite three Norwegian defenders converging on him. It was a sign of dismal Norway defending — Mbappé had already hit the bar inside 22 seconds against their makeshift back line — but also of the precise, lethal quality of France's attack. The switch of play to isolate Dembélé one-against-the-defence on the right would become the night's recurring, unstoppable motif.

⚽ Goal 2 (20') — The carbon copy from distance

France · 2-0Same space, same corner — this time from outside the box
Dembélé strike from distance

Even better than the first. Thirteen minutes later, Dembélé doubled his tally with a goal that was, if anything, more eye-catching: he found space between three defenders at the top of the box and fired a left-footer beyond Selvik's diving save into the same bottom corner. This is the essence of what made him unplayable — Norway knew exactly what he wanted to do (drift right, cut onto the left, shoot across goal), threw bodies at him to stop it, and simply couldn't. The combination of close control in tight space, the speed of the shift, and the precision of the strike is close to undefendable when a player is this in-form. Norway briefly responded — but France's machine was just warming up.

⚽ Goal 3 (32') — The treble, a third carbon copy

France · 3-1After Aasgaard's reply, Dembélé restores the two-goal cushion the only way he knows
Release into the right channel Dembélé cut inside & finish

The hat-trick — and the definition of in-form ruthlessness. Norway had pulled one back through Aasgaard, but the response was immediate and inevitable. Dembélé completed his treble by scoring a near-carbon copy of his second — the same surge into the right channel, the same cut onto the left foot, the same corner. Three goals, three variations on one unstoppable theme, all inside 32 minutes. It restored France's two-goal cushion and effectively killed the game as a contest. Whatever the quality of the opposition, scoring the same goal three times while the defence is expressly trying to stop that exact pattern is a rare display of individual brilliance — and a timely reminder that France's match-winner doesn't have to be Mbappé.

⚠️ The Warning Beneath the Brilliance

🚨 A galaxy of attack, a worryingly shaky defence

Here is the paradox of a 4-1 win: Norway's second-string actually had the higher xG — 1.69 to France's 1.31. For all that breathtaking flair, France conceded (Aasgaard's strike), gave away a second-half penalty (a lazy Théo Hernández trip on Bobb — only saved because Strand Larsen's spot-kick was weak and Maignan stopped it), and let Bobb in one-on-one (Maignan again). Against a reserve Norway side, that's a flashing red light. This is the team's defining flaw: a front line good enough to win the World Cup, attached to a defence that keeps inviting trouble through individual errors and a lack of transition discipline. If France are to lift the trophy, this must be fixed — and fast, because the knockout opponents won't fluff their penalties.

🛡️ Defensive Fix 1 — Full-back discipline & the rest-defence

France · Defensive Fix 1Stop the rash challenges; keep one full-back home
Jockey & shepherd (do) Lazy lunge (don't)

The most immediate, fixable problem. The penalty France conceded came from a lazy, rash Théo Hernández challenge on Bobb in the box — exactly the kind of needless individual error that gets punished in knockout football. Two disciplines must tighten. First, defend with the feet, not the lunge: in and around the box, France's defenders must jockey and shepherd attackers into harmless areas rather than diving in. Second, the rest-defence: with both full-backs (Koundé and Théo) bombing forward to support that elite attack, the channels behind them are repeatedly exposed — Senegal, and now even a reserve Norway, found joy there. Keeping one full-back disciplined and home-side while the other overlaps, with the pivot screening, closes the door without blunting the attack. France score so freely they can afford a fraction more caution; they cannot afford to keep gifting chances.

🧱 Defensive Fix 2 — Transition control: protect against the counter

France · Defensive Fix 2A screening pivot & counter-press to kill the break
Screen / counter-press Counter-pass denied

The structural fix — control the moment the ball is lost. France's brilliance going forward has a cost: when those fast, committed transitions break down, the team is stretched and vulnerable to the counter — which is how Norway's reserves manufactured their chances (Aasgaard's goal, Bobb's one-on-one). The remedy is twofold. First, an anchored screening pivot — Tchouaméni sitting in front of the centre-backs, never both pivots flying forward — to protect the central lane every counter wants to use. Second, an aggressive counter-press: the instant possession is lost high up, the nearest French player presses the ball immediately to kill the break at source rather than retreating and inviting a run at the exposed back line. France have the athletes (Koné, Tchouaméni) to do both. With Saliba due back to add composure, the talent is there to be the tournament's best defence as well as its best attack — but the discipline must match the flair. Get it right, and France are the team to beat.

🗺️ What Happens Next — Into the Knockouts

France top Group I with a flawless nine points, ten goals scored, and a hat-trick hero who has taken the creative weight off Mbappé's shoulders — and Didier Deschamps is set to rejoin the squad at training, his side having played with heart in his absence. The attacking ceiling is frighteningly high: when France move the ball this fast, with Dembélé, Mbappé and Olise interchanging, no defence looks safe. But the assistant Guy Stéphan said it himself — there were moments they "weren't quite at their best" that need ironing out, and the numbers (a reserve Norway out-xG'ing them) name the problem precisely. France enter the Round of 32 as genuine favourites, with a title-winning attack and a defence that could yet cost them everything. The knockouts will reward the side that fixes its flaw fastest.

📊 Bottom Line

A breathtaking first half and a historic hat-trick — Ousmane Dembélé's 32-minute treble was the most dazzling individual display of the World Cup so far, and the fluency of France's attacking transitions was a joy that no defence looks built to contain. But read past the 4-1: a reserve Norway side carried the higher xG, France conceded, gave away a penalty through a careless challenge, and rode their goalkeeper's saves. The attack is championship-calibre; the defence is the fault line that could undo it. France have the personnel to be complete — Saliba returning, athletes in midfield, a keeper in form — but the flair must be matched by discipline at the back. Fix that, and this is a team that wins the World Cup. Leave it unaddressed, and a sharper opponent will punish what Norway's reserves could not.
