🧭 The Story of the Match

For 45 minutes, the script looked dangerously like 2002 all over again. France started nervously, and Senegal were the better team for most of the first half — physical, organised, urgent in the duels, and genuinely threatening on the break. The Lions of Teranga had the two best chances of the half: Nicolas Jackson beat Upamecano on a lightning counter (after Mbappé lost possession) and rattled the post, and right before the interval Ismaïla Sarr somehow missed from close range after a Mané cutback — the kind of gilt-edged opening that, had it gone in, might have flipped the entire tournament narrative. That Senegal couldn't convert their half-chances exposed their one true limitation — a lack of ruthless end product — and underlined the experience of France's four-man defence, which bent without breaking.

🔎 On Mbappé's wretched first half

The captain was, in the words of one broadcaster, "dreadful" before the break — mistiming at least three touches, two of them in the box off promising passes, and losing possession to gift Jackson that early chance. The most plausible read isn't decline but rhythm: a player adjusting to the unique tempo and intensity of a World Cup opener, his timing a fraction off after the long season. Crucially, France as a whole lacked any method for creating space — everyone wanted to be the No.10, with Mbappé, Dembélé, Doué and Olise all drifting into the same congested central pockets, producing a confused scramble in the final third rather than penetration. The famous front three's single combined touch before half-time is the statistical fingerprint of that disconnection.

🎩 The Coach Who Reads Games — Deschamps' Half-Time Masterstroke

This is where rare pedigree told. Didier Deschamps is one of only three men to win the World Cup as both captain (1998) and coach (2018) — and his in-game intelligence decided this match. He made no personnel change at the break; instead he reorganised the shape. France stretched the pitch — pushing width and asking players to occupy the touchlines rather than clogging the middle — which instantly manufactured the space that had been absent. Olise drifted central to control midfield alongside Rabiot, France pressed higher to force Senegal errors, and the congestion cleared.

The crucial nuance: Senegal, to their immense credit, never sat back and parked the bus the way Cape Verde did to Spain a day earlier — they kept attacking, kept committing bodies forward. That bravery is exactly what Deschamps read at the interval: a side that keeps pushing leaves space behind it. By stretching the field and going vertical, France turned Senegal's admirable ambition into the very gaps Mbappé would exploit. The change was as much about reading the opponent as fixing his own team.

⚽ Goal of the Match 1 — Mbappé's opener (66')

France · 1-0Olise's curled pass & the one-motion finish
Olise's curled through-ball Mbappé's run Finish

The breakthrough, and a product of the tactical reset. With France now stretching the pitch and Senegal still committed forward, the gaps appeared. Michael Olise — superb all night, and the game's most creative force — curled a pass around four Senegalese defenders into the one corridor that mattered. Mbappé, finally finding his rhythm, met it and turned and shot in a single motion, leaving Koulibaly for dead and slipping it past Mendy. It was the first goal of France's tournament and the moment the floodgates opened. The contrast with the first half was total: where France had forced the ball into traffic before the break, here they played through a stretched defence — exactly what Deschamps' reorganisation was designed to produce.

🚀 Goal of the Match 2 — Mbappé's 30-yard thunderbolt (90+6')

France · 3-1The coronation strike from distance
Mbappé's strike (30 yds) Goalkeeper's late dive

The goal that crowned a king. Barely a minute after Ibrahim Mbaye's brilliant solo strike had pulled Senegal back to 2-1 and injected late tension, Mbappé answered instantly. He collected the ball fully 30 yards out, was allowed a half-yard as tired defenders backed off, and lifted a ferocious strike into the top-left corner — over Mendy and just under the bar. Zlatan Ibrahimović called it "a magic moment." It made Mbappé France's all-time leading scorer (58) and moved him past Messi on the all-time World Cup list. One honest caveat the highlight reels won't dwell on: for all the undeniable power and technique, Mendy's dive was late and mistimed — he leapt a fraction slow and got nowhere near a ball a top keeper might have reached. It's the kind of small goalkeeping error that Cape Verde's Vozinha never granted Spain the day before — a reminder that fine margins, not just brilliance, separate the teams who win from the teams who draw.

📋 Key Takeaways

  • Mbappé (after the break) — from dreadful to decisive. Two goals, a record, and a statement of why he's among the greatest of all time. The first half is the worry; the second is the reminder.
  • Michael Olise — France's best player. His central drift and defence-splitting passing created the opener and several other chances. A genuine creative hub.
  • Bradley Barcola — 131 seconds to score on his World Cup debut. France's attacking depth is frightening.
  • Senegal — brave, organised, and superior for a half, but undone by missed chances and Mbappé's class. No shame, and a warning to the group.
  • Ibrahim Mbaye (18) — the youngest African scorer in World Cup history, a star in the making, billed as "the next Mbappé."

🔧 France's Future — Fix 1: Solve the "everyone's a No.10" problem

France · Fix 1Stagger the front four — fix the roles, keep the width
Width → service The single No.10 feeds Mbappé

The first-half lesson made permanent. France's early struggles came from four creators crowding the same central zone — a scramble of would-be No.10s with nobody stretching the pitch. The second-half fix worked because it imposed roles: Dembélé and Olise stay genuinely wide to pin full-backs and open the channels; one player (Doué) is the designated No.10 rather than three; and Mbappé holds the highest line as the focal point of the runs, not another deep-dropping playmaker. Against Iraq and Norway — and the knockouts — Deschamps should start in this shape rather than rediscovering it at half-time. The talent is overwhelming; the discipline of who occupies which lane is what turns it from a scramble into a machine.

🛡️ France's Future — Fix 2: Protect the full-back zones & start faster

France · Fix 2A screening pivot so the counters don't bite
Screening / cover Opponent counter denied

The vulnerability Senegal exposed — and the next opponent will too. Jackson's post and Sarr's sitter both came from the same source: France's full-backs pushing high and leaving the channel behind them open to fast transitions, with too little protection. Mbappé's well-documented reluctance to press compounds it. The fix is structural discipline: Tchouaméni sitting as a genuine screening pivot in front of the back line, and a clear rule that when one full-back bombs forward (Theo), the other (Koundé) tucks in to keep a back-three shape in rest-defence. Add a faster start — Deschamps' sides are chronically slow openers, and against Iraq's likely deep block that's less punishing, but against a Norway side with Haaland it could be fatal. France can't rely on a Mbappé rescue act every game; tightening the transitions is how a good team becomes a winning one.

🗺️ What Happens Next

Three points, top of the group, and Mbappé purring — France could hardly have asked for more from a game that, at half-time, was threatening to become a nightmare. But the performance carried clear warnings: the slow start, the congested attacking shape, and the soft transition defending are all fixable, and all must be fixed before the knockouts. The good news is the next test, Iraq, will likely sit deep — a chance to rehearse the disciplined, lane-defined attacking shape against a low block (the very problem that humbled Spain). The sterner exam is Norway and Haaland on Matchday 3, possibly for top spot, where the transition defending will be ruthlessly probed.

📊 Bottom Line

France won ugly, then won beautifully — a sleepy, scrambled first half rescued by a coach reading the game perfectly and a superstar answering the call. Deschamps' half-time reorganisation, not a personnel change, turned the match; Senegal's bravery in keeping the game open played into French hands; and Mbappé's brilliance (with a small assist from a mistimed goalkeeper dive) did the rest. The result is ideal, but the diagnosis is honest: fix the attacking roles, protect the full-back channels, and start faster. Get those right, and a France side this talented is a serious bet to reach a third straight World Cup final. The pieces are all here — Deschamps just has to make them click from the first whistle, not the 46th minute.
