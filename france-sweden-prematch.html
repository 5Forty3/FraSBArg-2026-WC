🎯 The context — a heavy favourite at the home of the final

On paper, a mismatch — and the venue could not be more fitting. France begin their knockout run at MetLife Stadium, the very ground that will host the final on July 19, as heavy favourites (around -400) against a Sweden side that scraped in as one of the eight best third-placed teams. France were imperious in Group I — perfect nine points, 10 goals, their first flawless group since the 1998 triumph — powered by the most fearsome attack in the tournament. Sweden, by contrast, were unconvincing: a false-dawn win over Tunisia, a chastening 5-1 hammering by the Netherlands, and a 1-1 draw with Japan, arriving with no clean sheet in 14 matches. Graham Potter's side are playing with house money. But knockout football allows no second chances — and France know it.

📋 Team News XIs predicted · key news confirmed

The big stories are confirmed: Deschamps is back on the touchline, Saliba is expected to return, and Sweden have lost a key defender. Exact XIs are predicted (not officially confirmed), but the picture is clear.

🇫🇷 France — the tournament's deepest attack (expected XI)

  • Deschamps back on the touchline (confirmed) — the manager returned home during the group stage following the death of his mother, and is back to lead the side. He has already confirmed he will step down after the tournament — a final World Cup tilt.
  • Saliba expected to return (confirmed fit-ish) — rested against Norway with a back issue, the Arsenal centre-back has trained and is probable to start, restoring the elite Saliba-Upamecano pairing. Crucial against Sweden's strikers.
  • Thuram out — a calf issue rules him out again, but France's attacking embarrassment of riches barely notices.
  • Dembélé in red-hot form — his first-half hat-trick against Norway (4 group goals) means France's threat extends well beyond Mbappé; Olise (3 assists) is the creative hub, Doué and Barcola competing for the last forward spot.
  • The double pivot: Tchouaméni and Rabiot screen the back four — Deschamps's preferred balance, freeing the front four.

Expected XI (4-2-3-1): Maignan; Koundé, Upamecano, Saliba, T. Hernández; Tchouaméni, Rabiot; Dembélé, Olise, Doué; Mbappé. (Not confirmed.)

🇸🇪 Sweden — two world-class strikers, a shaky platform

  • Isak Hien OUT (confirmed) — the centre-back suffered a hamstring injury against Japan and is done for the tournament. A significant blow to an already-leaky defence.
  • The strike pair is everything: Alexander Isak and Viktor Gyökeres are a Premier League-calibre duo who can hurt anyone — Sweden's entire hope rests on getting them service in an open game.
  • Potter's back three: Lindelöf returns to a back line that has conceded freely (7 goals in 3 group games, 5 of them to the Dutch). The young Bergvall-Ayari pivot must protect it.
  • Playing with freedom — no expectations after a winless qualifying campaign salvaged by a playoff. Dangerous precisely because they have nothing to lose.

Projected XI (3-4-3): Zetterström; Lagerbielke, Lindelöf, Gudmundsson; Bernhardsson, Bergvall, Ayari, Stroud; Elanga, Gyökeres, Isak. (Not confirmed.)

⚙️ The Tactical Battle — Will Sweden open the game up?

The entire contest hinges on one question: can Sweden resist the temptation to chase the game, or will they get drawn into the open, transition-heavy track meet where France are simply unstoppable? France are at their most lethal when space appears behind a high line — exactly what happens when Sweden push men up to feed Gyökeres and Isak. France's one (slight) vulnerability is slow, settled possession against a deep, compact block; Sweden's only realistic path is to sit deep, stay patient, and hope their two stars can nick a chance on the break. But with a makeshift defence missing Hien, holding France out for 90 minutes is a tall order.

🔑 France's Key — Mbappé & the runners attack the space behind

France · AttackVertical balls in behind a high, shaky Swedish line
Olise's release pass Mbappé run in behind

France's most direct route to a comfortable evening. Sweden's defence is shaky, now missing Isak Hien, and will likely have to push up at some point to support its strikers — and the moment any space appears behind that back three, France's runners are lethal. The mechanism is simple and unstoppable: Olise or Tchouaméni picking out the vertical pass for Mbappé (and Dembélé) to attack the channels. Mbappé chasing a through-ball against a high, makeshift line is the single most dangerous picture in this tournament — pure pace turning a half-yard into a finish. France's whole game is built to create exactly this, and Sweden's profile (forced to commit men forward for Gyökeres and Isak) plays right into it. If the game opens up even slightly, France could win it at a canter.

🗡️ Sweden's Hope — Gyökeres & Isak in a low-block-and-counter

Sweden · The Upset PlanStay compact, then spring the two strikers on the break
Transition release Isak / Gyökeres runs

Sweden's only realistic route to a shock. Graham Potter knows his side can't out-football France — so the plan must be disciplined and ruthless: sit in a compact mid-to-low block, frustrate France's slower build-up, and rely on the one area where they have genuine world-class quality — the Isak-Gyökeres strike pair on the counter. France's one vulnerability, hinted at all tournament (the reserve Norway out-xG'd them, they've conceded cheaply), is the transition moment when their full-backs and midfield push high. If Sweden can win the ball and release Isak into the channel or Gyökeres through the middle before France reset, two Premier League-calibre finishers will get chances. It requires near-perfect discipline for 90 minutes and a ruthless take of a rare opening — but with these two strikers, it is not impossible. France must respect the break.

🎯 The Duel That Decides It — Saliba & Upamecano vs Isak & Gyökeres

The Key BattleThe tournament's best young CB pair vs two elite strikers
Service to strikers Smothered by the CB pair

The single matchup that could keep Sweden in it — and why Saliba's fitness mattered so much. Sweden's hope lives entirely through Isak and Gyökeres, which makes the central-defensive duel decisive. William Saliba and Dayot Upamecano are arguably the best young centre-back pairing in the tournament — quick, strong, composed — and France rested Saliba precisely so he'd be available here. Their job is clear: stay tight, deny Isak and Gyökeres the turn, and win the physical and aerial duels so the two strikers never get a clean look. If France's elite pair smother the only genuine threat Sweden carry, there is simply no other route to goal for Potter's side — and the tie becomes a procession. If Saliba is below 100% and the pair give the strikers even half a yard, Sweden suddenly have a puncher's chance. Win this duel, win the game.

📊 Verdict & Predicted Scoreline

This has the makings of a statement performance. France are the most complete attacking force in the tournament, in perfect form, with Dembélé and Mbappé sharing the goalscoring load and an elite defensive spine restored by Saliba's return. Sweden have two strikers capable of a moment of magic — and that, plus France's habit of starting slowly, means an early scare or a transition goal can't be dismissed. But the gulf is vast: Sweden's defence is shaky and short-handed without Hien, their qualifying and group form was poor, and holding this French attack scoreless for 90 minutes is beyond almost anyone. Expect France to weather any early Swedish ambition, then pull clear once the runners get in behind. The favourites advance, and likely in some style — though Isak or Gyökeres may grab a consolation. Allez les Bleus.

🇫🇷 France 3 — 1 Sweden

Most likely: France's pace in behind proves decisive, with Mbappé and Dembélé among the goals. Sweden's elite strike pair offer a consolation and a nervy moment — but only one outcome is realistic.
